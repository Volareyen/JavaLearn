### **Stage 3: The Four Pillars of Wisdom - The Core of OOP**

*You have forged a simple thing. Now, you will learn the four great truths that give OOP its power and elegance. Master these, and the world of complex systems will open to you.*

*   **`03-The_Four_Pillars/`**
    *   **`01-Pillar_I_Encapsulation/`**: (The Sacred Vault)
        *   `README.md`: The principle of hiding complexity. Bundling data (fields) and the methods that operate on that data together. Using `private` access modifiers to protect an object's internal state. The "why" behind getters and setters. Analogy: A magical amulet whose inner workings are hidden, but whose power can be accessed through specific chants (public methods).
    *   **`02-Pillar_II_Inheritance/`**: (The Bloodline)
        *   `README.md`: The "is-a" relationship. Creating new classes from existing ones. The `extends` keyword. Superclass (parent) and Subclass (child). The `super` keyword to call upon the parent's wisdom. **Method Overriding** (`@Override`) to specialize behavior. This is the pillar of reusability. Analogy: A `GoldenRetriever` *is-a* `Dog`, which *is-a* `Animal`. It inherits traits but has its own unique qualities.
    *   **`03-Pillar_III_Polymorphism/`**: (The Art of Many Forms)
        *   `README.md`: The most profound pillar. An object's ability to take on many forms. A variable of a superclass type can refer to an object of any of its subclasses. A single action (`animal.makeSound()`) can have different outcomes depending on the object's true nature (`Dog` barks, `Cat` meows). This pillar decouples the "what" from the "how." We will also cover **Method Overloading** (same method name, different parameters) here as a form of compile-time polymorphism.
    *   **`04-Pillar_IV_Abstraction/`**: (The Unseen Essence)
        *   `README.md`: Hiding implementation details and showing only essential features.
        *   **`a-Abstract_Classes/`**: Creating classes that cannot be instantiated, serving as a template for other classes. A mix of concrete methods and `abstract` methods (methods with no body, a promise that children must fulfill). Analogy: The *concept* of a `Vehicle`. You can't build a generic "Vehicle," but you can build a `Car` or a `Bicycle` which are concrete forms of it.
        *   **`b-Interfaces/`**: A pure contract. A collection of abstract methods (and constants). A class `implements` an interface, promising to provide the behavior defined within. This is the ultimate tool for decoupling. Analogy: A USB port. It doesn't care if you plug in a mouse, a keyboard, or a lamp; as long as the device `implements` the USB "contract," it will work.
